What GDPS Means

GDPS stands for Governance Decisions Per Second. One governance decision is a single transaction evaluated against the full active policy set. Not one rule. All 29 policies, applied to one transaction, simultaneously. The result is a complete governance verdict — not a partial check.

We use "governance decisions" rather than "compliance events" because the engine does more than flag transactions against static rules. Each decision incorporates full entity graph context, multi-policy evaluation, and produces a cryptographic proof bundle. It is a governed verdict, not an event counter.

This is an important distinction from how other vendors report throughput. Some report transactions per second, where each transaction is checked against one rule at a time in sequence. If you have 29 rules, sequential evaluation means your effective policy throughput is 29 times lower than the headline transaction rate.

In the ZQUAS engine, the GPU evaluates all policies against each transaction in parallel. One GPU block per transaction. One thread per policy. The 2.58 million GDPS figure means 2.58 million transactions were each evaluated against all 29 policies simultaneously. That's over 74 million individual policy evaluations per second.

2.58M transactions × 29 policies = 74.8M individual policy evaluations per second. On a single GPU.

How It Was Measured

The benchmark uses the Themis Governance Benchmark (TGB v1.2), a standalone harness that measures throughput, latency, determinism, and cryptographic proof generation under controlled conditions.

Transactions are synthetically generated with realistic parameter distributions (amount, currency, jurisdiction, counterparty type, temporal spacing). The generator produces a continuous stream at a rate exceeding the engine's processing capacity to ensure the benchmark measures engine throughput, not data generation throughput.

Timing uses CUDA events for GPU-side measurement, eliminating host-side timing noise. The measurement window excludes startup and warmdown periods. The reported figure is sustained throughput over the measurement window, not a burst or peak.

The benchmark harness is a standalone executable (TgbBench) that runs independently of the full engine application. It isolates the policy evaluation pipeline: GPU context packet serialisation, kernel dispatch, verdict collection, and epoch commitment. It does not include data ingestion, entity graph updates, or rendering overhead. These subsystems operate concurrently in the full engine but are excluded from the policy evaluation benchmark to measure the adjudication pipeline in isolation.

Why This Matters for Banks

A Tier-1 bank processes roughly 5 to 50 million transactions per day, depending on business mix and payment types. At 2.58 million governance decisions per second, the engine can evaluate a full day's transaction volume in under 20 seconds. With the full policy set. Against every transaction. No sampling. No prioritisation. No overnight batch window.

For real-time payment rails (SEPA Instant, Faster Payments, RTP), the relevant metric is whether the engine can evaluate a transaction before settlement completes. SEPA Instant requires settlement in under 10 seconds. At sub-millisecond batch latency, the engine evaluates the transaction in a fraction of the settlement window.

For banks considering GPU-based compliance, the hardware cost is relevant. An NVIDIA RTX 5090 costs approximately €2,000 to €2,500 at retail. The entire benchmark was run on a single consumer-grade GPU. Enterprise GPUs (A100, H100) would provide higher throughput and larger VRAM, but the benchmark demonstrates that enterprise-class compliance performance is achievable on hardware that costs less than a single compliance analyst's monthly salary.

One GPU. One month's analyst salary. 2.58 million governance decisions per second. The economics of GPU-native compliance are difficult to argue with.

Detection Fidelity: 1.25M Transaction Simulation

Separately from the throughput benchmark, a detection fidelity test simulated 1.25 million transactions modelled on Tier-1 bank transaction patterns, including known suspicious patterns based on published enforcement actions and FATF typologies.

The detection fidelity benchmark measures whether the policy set correctly identifies known suspicious patterns while avoiding false triggers on legitimate transaction patterns. Results are measured in precision (what percentage of alerts were genuine) and recall (what percentage of genuine suspicious patterns were detected).

Detailed detection fidelity results are available under NDA for qualified evaluation partners. The benchmark framework (HsbcDetectionReport) is available for inspection during technical due diligence.

Verification

The benchmark is deterministic. Running TGB v1.2 with the same policy set on the same hardware produces identical results. The benchmark executable is subject to the same build attestation as the main engine: embedded BLAKE3 and SHA-256 hashes, compiler version, git commit, and Ed25519 signature.

The benchmark can be independently verified by running the TgbBench executable with the provided policy set and comparing results against the published figures. Hardware differences will affect absolute throughput but not determinism or correctness.

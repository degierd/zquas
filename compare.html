Reading this table

This comparison reflects architectural capabilities, not product quality. Legacy incumbents have deep integration with bank infrastructure, decades of regulatory familiarity, and thousands of production deployments. AI-native challengers have modern interfaces, strong ML capabilities, and significant venture funding. These are real advantages.

The differences shown above are structural. They reflect what each architecture can and cannot do, not what each company chooses to prioritise. A CPU-based cloud platform cannot offer GPU-native processing through a software update. A centralised SaaS platform cannot offer privacy-preserving MPC without fundamental re-architecture. A system without cryptographic attestation cannot produce independently verifiable proof bundles by adding a feature.

These are not criticisms. They are architectural facts. The question for each institution is which architectural properties matter for their specific compliance requirements, regulatory jurisdiction, and risk appetite.

Legacy Incumbents

NICE Actimize · Oracle Financial Crime · SAS

These vendors own the installed base at most Tier-1 and Tier-2 banks globally. Their systems are deeply integrated into bank infrastructure with 15 to 20 integration points per deployment. They have extensive regulatory familiarity across multiple jurisdictions. Switching away from them is expensive and risky, which is their primary competitive advantage. Their architectural limitation is that they were designed for batch processing on CPU infrastructure. Retrofitting GPU compute, MPC, or cryptographic attestation onto these platforms would require multi-year re-engineering efforts that conflict with the needs of their existing production customers.

AI-Native Challengers

Sardine ($145M raised) · Hawk AI ($56M raised) · Featurespace · ComplyAdvantage

These companies represent the modern wave of compliance technology. Cloud-first, API-driven, with machine learning for alert scoring and increasingly, agentic AI for alert triage. They are winning fintech and mid-market bank customers and are beginning to penetrate Tier-1 institutions. Their architectural limitation is that they are better versions of the existing paradigm: CPU-based cloud processing with ML models. They do not offer GPU-native processing, privacy-preserving cross-institutional detection, or cryptographic decision proofs. They compete on features and UX within the existing architectural framework.

ZQUAS

Pre-revenue · DNB InnovationHub · FCA Sandbox

ZQUAS is architecturally different, not incrementally better. The engine runs on GPU, processes 2.58 million governance decisions per second, enforces policies deterministically via compiled bytecode, and produces cryptographic proof bundles for every decision. Cross-institutional detection is architected via GPU-accelerated MPC with zero raw data disclosure, validated in controlled testing. The trade-off is maturity: ZQUAS is pre-revenue with no production deployments. It has regulatory sandbox engagement but not production-scale validation. The architecture is proven in benchmark environments but not yet in a live bank. This page does not hide that fact.

Where ZQUAS is behind

Transparency matters. Here is where existing vendors have clear advantages over ZQUAS today:

Production deployments

Legacy vendors have thousands. AI-native challengers have hundreds. ZQUAS has zero. Production validation is the single most important factor in bank procurement decisions.

Regulatory familiarity

Regulators know NICE Actimize and Oracle. They have seen them in hundreds of examinations. ZQUAS is in sandbox engagement. That is early-stage credibility, not established trust.

Professional services

Large vendors have implementation teams, customer success organisations, and 24/7 support. ZQUAS is a solo founder. Implementation support at scale requires a team that doesn't exist yet.

Ecosystem integration

Legacy vendors have pre-built connectors to every major core banking system, payment processor, and case management platform. ZQUAS has standard format ingestion and export but not vendor-specific connectors.

These gaps are real. They close with time, traction, and investment. The architectural gaps in the other direction do not close with time. They require re-architecture.

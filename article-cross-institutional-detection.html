Why Cross-Institutional AML Detection Requires a New Architecture

Joint transaction monitoring initiatives across Europe have demonstrated both the necessity and the impossibility of centralised data pooling. The problem isn't willingness — it's architecture.

The Detection Gap Is Real

An estimated €16 billion in criminal money is laundered through the Netherlands annually. Across the EU, the figures run into hundreds of billions. Banks collectively spend over €200 billion per year on financial crime compliance. Yet by most estimates, less than 1% of illicit funds are successfully intercepted.

The reason is structural. Money laundering networks operate across institutions. A criminal deposits cash at Bank A, transfers to a shell company at Bank B, purchases real estate through Bank C, and repatriates funds via Bank D. Each bank sees a single, seemingly unremarkable transaction. None sees the pattern.

This is not a failure of technology or talent. It is a consequence of architecture. When each institution monitors only its own customers, cross-bank criminal networks are invisible by design.

Why Data Centralisation Failed

The logical response — pooling transaction data across institutions for joint analysis — has been tried. Multiple European jurisdictions have explored or implemented shared monitoring utilities. The results have been instructive.

The challenge is not technical. The challenge is legal and political. Centralising raw transaction data from multiple banks into a single entity creates a surveillance infrastructure that conflicts with fundamental rights under GDPR. Privacy advocates and civil liberties organisations have rightly objected. The EU's new Anti-Money Laundering Regulation (AMLR), effective mid-2027, explicitly restricts inter-bank data sharing to situations involving pre-identified high-risk customers — effectively prohibiting the centralised pooling model for broad-based detection.

The core paradox: effective detection requires cross-institutional visibility, but data centralisation — the most intuitive path to that visibility — violates the privacy principles that democratic societies require.

This is not a policy failure. AMLR reflects a legitimate democratic judgement that mass financial surveillance, even for anti-crime purposes, must be bounded. Any viable solution must respect this constraint, not circumvent it.

A Different Architectural Premise

The assumption underlying centralised monitoring is that you need to see the data to analyse it. This assumption is false. Advances in privacy-preserving computation — specifically secure multi-party computation (MPC) and zero-knowledge proofs — allow meaningful analysis across datasets that never leave their respective institutions.

In an MPC-based architecture, each bank retains full sovereignty over its own data. No transaction record leaves the bank's perimeter. Instead, the system computes risk comparisons cryptographically: Bank A and Bank B can jointly determine whether a shared counterparty exhibits suspicious patterns across both institutions — without either bank revealing its underlying transactions to the other.

Dimension Centralised Model MPC-Based Model

Privacy exposure Full transaction visibility Zero raw data disclosure

Performance Is No Longer the Barrier

MPC protocols have historically been dismissed as impractical for real-time financial applications due to computational overhead. This objection was valid five years ago. It is no longer.

GPU-accelerated MPC — running cryptographic comparison, oblivious sorting, and secure aggregation on modern GPU architectures — achieves throughput levels compatible with real-time transaction monitoring. The same GPU parallelism that powers AI training and high-frequency trading can execute privacy-preserving financial crime detection at scale.

When combined with GPU-native policy evaluation, a single node can process millions of compliance events per second while running the complete policy set against every transaction. There is no longer a trade-off between detection breadth and computational feasibility.

Verifiability Changes the Regulatory Relationship

Perhaps the most significant architectural shift is in auditability. In legacy compliance systems, regulators rely on log files, case management records, and institutional self-reporting to assess whether monitoring systems are functioning correctly. This is a trust-based model.

A cryptographically attested compliance architecture replaces trust with verification. Each batch of compliance decisions produces a sealed evidence bundle: a cryptographic proof that specific policies were applied to specific data and produced specific verdicts. The regulator can independently verify this proof using a standalone tool — without accessing the bank's systems, without trusting the vendor's software, and without seeing the underlying transaction data.

This transforms the supervisory relationship. Instead of asking "show me your monitoring reports," the regulator asks "give me your proof bundle." Verification becomes deterministic rather than interpretive.

What Comes Next

The EU AMLR creates both the constraint and the opportunity. By restricting centralised data pooling, it forces the industry toward privacy-preserving alternatives. By establishing a pan-European legal framework for inter-institutional cooperation, it creates the regulatory foundation for MPC-based detection networks.

The technology exists. The regulatory framework is emerging. The institutional will — demonstrated by years of investment in joint monitoring initiatives — is clearly present. What is needed is an architecture that satisfies all three simultaneously: detection efficacy, data sovereignty, and regulatory verifiability.

That architecture is no longer theoretical.
